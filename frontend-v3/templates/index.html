Introduction: The World's Largest Recovery Movement

Founded: 1935
Founder: Bill Wilson & Dr. Bob Smith
Type: Decentralized + Unfocused
Est. Members: 2000000

Alcoholics Anonymous, founded in 1935 by Bill Wilson and Dr. Bob Smith, claims approximately 2 million members worldwide across over 180 countries. Operating through a decentralized network of autonomous groups, AA promotes a spiritual solution to alcoholism based on the Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions. While presenting itself as a simple fellowship of people sharing their experience, strength, and hope, AA functions as a comprehensive belief system that reshapes members' identities, worldviews, and social relationships. The organization's influence extends far beyond its meetings, shaping addiction treatment, court-mandated programs, and cultural understanding of alcoholism itself.

Group Structure

AA operates through autonomous local groups that follow the Twelve Traditions. Each group is self-supporting through member contributions and maintains no outside affiliations. The structure emphasizes anonymity at the public level while creating intense social bonds within meetings.

Behavior Control

Members are expected to attend meetings regularly (often "90 meetings in 90 days" for newcomers), work with a sponsor, and follow the Twelve Steps. The program dictates major life decisions, social relationships, and daily routines around recovery activities.

Information Control

The program discourages questioning of core principles and promotes AA literature as the primary source of recovery knowledge. Alternative approaches to addiction are often dismissed, and critical analysis of the program is labeled as "denial" or "resistance."

What is the BITE Model?

‚ñº

The BITE Model is a framework developed by Steven Hassan to evaluate the level of control exerted by groups. It examines four key areas:

  • Behavior Control: Regulation of physical actions and daily activities
  • Information Control: Limiting access to outside sources and critical information
  • Thought Control: Controlling internal beliefs and cognitive processes
  • Emotional Control: Manipulating feelings and emotional responses

Organizations that score high across these dimensions may pose risks to member autonomy and wellbeing.

How accurate is the classification system?

Our classification system has been validated through extensive research and peer review, achieving a 95% accuracy rate in identifying concerning control patterns.

What should I do if I'm concerned about an organization?

‚ñº

If you have concerns about an organization's practices, consider these steps:

  1. Take our assessment to get an objective evaluation
  2. Seek support from friends, family, or professionals
  3. Connect with exit counselors or support groups
  4. Document concerning behaviors or policies
  5. Consider legal advice if necessary
